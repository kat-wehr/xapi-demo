Assessment · 4 Minutes In

Oxygen is on. Now — what do you listen for?

SpO₂ has improved to 95% on 2L nasal cannula. Maya is still visibly working to breathe but appears slightly calmer. You're ready to auscultate.

On lung auscultation you hear diffuse expiratory wheezing bilaterally, more prominent in the right lower lobe. The inspiratory phase sounds are clear. She has a mild but audible wheeze even without your stethoscope.

The parent mentions: "She had something like this once before — they gave her a breathing treatment."

✓Strong clinical reasoning. Bilateral expiratory wheezing with a history of prior bronchodilator response strongly suggests reactive airway disease. Albuterol is first-line. Notifying the physician while preparing the treatment is appropriate teamwork.
△Diagnostically cautious, but delays treatment. While imaging can be useful, it shouldn't delay bronchodilator therapy when the presentation is classic for reactive airway disease. The asymmetric right lower lobe finding warrants mention to the physician, but treatment comes first.
✕Oxygen alone is insufficient here. The underlying bronchospasm is not being treated. SpO₂ improving on supplemental O₂ doesn't mean the pathology is resolving — it means you're compensating for it. She needs bronchodilator therapy.

⚠ Deterioration · 12 Minutes In

Maya is getting worse — fast

Despite your interventions so far, Maya has deteriorated. SpO₂ has dropped to 87%. She is now showing subcostal retractions in addition to intercostal. Her GCS has dropped to 13 — she's becoming less responsive and her color is pale with perioral mottling.

The albuterol nebulizer has been running for 3 minutes. The physician has just arrived at bedside and looks to you for your recommendation.

✓Appropriate escalation. Non-rebreather mask dramatically increases FiO₂. IV magnesium sulfate is evidence-based for severe pediatric asthma exacerbation when initial bronchodilators are insufficient. This is the right next step before considering intubation.
△Not wrong, but premature. PICU notification is appropriate soon, but intubation in a severe asthmatic child carries significant risk and should be a last resort. There are escalation steps — non-rebreather O₂, IV magnesium, heliox — that should come first.
✕This child cannot wait. A declining GCS with SpO₂ at 87% and worsening retractions is a deteriorating airway. Continuing current therapy without escalation risks respiratory arrest. Immediate intervention is required.

Communication · Stabilization Phase

Maya is stabilizing. Her parent is terrified.

The escalated interventions are working. SpO₂ is back to 94% and Maya's GCS has normalized. She's on high-flow O₂ and the physician has ordered admission to the PICU for observation.

Maya's parent — who has been in the room the entire time — is shaking. They say: "Is she going to be okay? I don't understand what's happening. Did I wait too long to bring her in?"

You have approximately 90 seconds before the PICU transport team arrives.

✓Excellent patient-family communication. Honest, calm, and appropriately scoped for the time available. You're not withholding information — you're being realistic about what can be communicated meaningfully in 90 seconds under stress.
△Right intention, wrong moment. Full disclosure is important, but a panicked parent in 90 seconds is unlikely to retain a detailed clinical briefing. Prioritize emotional reassurance and a clear handoff — the PICU team will have time for a thorough family conversation.
✕Avoid false reassurance. Maya is being admitted to the PICU — telling a parent "there's nothing to worry about" is inaccurate and erodes trust when reality doesn't match. Honest, calm communication — even when brief — is always the right approach.
